# FDA Laser Light Show Safety - Digested Content

**Source:** U.S. Food and Drug Administration  
**Publication:** HHS Publication FDA 86-8262  
**Revised:** May 1986  
**Pages:** 34

---

## 1. Background: Understanding Laser Light

### The Nature of Laser Radiation
- Laser light is part of electromagnetic spectrum
- Falls between ultraviolet and infrared
- **Visible light:** Violet (short waves) to Red (long waves)
- Entertainment lasers use visible radiation
- Some lasers also emit UV or IR (invisible)

### Key Properties of Laser Light

| Property | Description | Safety Implication |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Monochromatic** | Single wavelength/pure color | Highly concentrated energy |
| **Coherent** | Waves in phase, synchronized | Maintains intensity over distance |
| **Low Divergence** | Beam stays narrow | Power remains concentrated |
| **High Intensity** | Thousands of times brighter than sunlight | Can cause instant injury |

### Reflection Hazards
- Reflected laser beams maintain almost full power
- Mirror balls can split beam into many lower-power rays
- Rough surfaces scatter but high-power lasers still dangerous
- Water, glass, metal, glossy floors can reflect hazardous beams

---

## 2. Laser Hazards

### Ocular Hazards
- Eye lens concentrates light onto retina
- Intensity increased 10,000x or more at retina
- **Peripheral vision hits:** Damage may not be noticed immediately
- **Direct hits:** Noticeable blind spots or serious impairment
- Damage can occur in a fraction of a second
- **Often painless** - victim may not realize injury occurred

### Skin Hazards
- Severe burns possible
- Some lasers can light cigarettes (imagine effect on skin)

### Hazard Determination
> "There is a hazard whenever a high power laser beam could possibly strike someone, particularly in the eyes."

## 3. FDA Regulatory Framework

### 21 CFR 1040 - Laser Performance Standard

All laser products made since August 1976 must comply.

### Laser Classification System

| Class | Power Output | Hazard Level |
|-------|--------------|--------------|
| **Class I** | <0.39 µW (microwatts) | No biological damage |
| **Class II** | <1 mW | Eye damage after long direct exposure |
| **Class IIIa** | 1-5 mW visible | Direct beam exposure injurious |
| **Class IIIb** | 5-500 mW | Immediate tissue injury |
| **Class IV** | >500 mW | Injury from direct, reflected, or scattered beams |

### Labeling Requirements
- All products above Class I must have class labels
- Additional safety features required by class

## 4. Variance Requirements for Light Shows

### When a Variance is Required
**Required for Class IIIb or IV lasers used in:**
- Performances
- Demonstrations  
- Entertainment purposes

### Who Needs a Variance?
"Manufacturers" includes:
- People who make laser products
- People who design/assemble/modify laser projectors
- Musical groups assembling their own shows
- Anyone setting up a show in a particular location
- **NOT** required for every individual show, but for each type of show

### Variance Application Requirements
- Submit report describing laser products
- Describe compliance methodology
- Submit BEFORE performance

### Safety Criteria for Variance Approval

1. Laser meets design/labeling requirements for its class
2. **Radiation cannot exceed Class I limits at audience locations**
   - Achieved via: mirror balls, scanning devices, other safeguards
3. **Scanning safeguards required** for reflected beams
4. **Performers** cannot exceed Class I limits if they must view beams
5. **Unattended lasers:** Restrict beams to >6m above, >2.5m from audience
6. **Attended lasers:** Restrict beams to >3m above, >2.5m from audience
7. Designated Laser Safety Officer with shutdown authority

### Notification Requirements

| Entity | Timing | Method |
|--------|--------|--------|
| FDA | 1 month advance | Written (phone if last-minute) |
| State/Local | In advance | Written (phone if urgent) |
| FAA (outdoor) | 2-4 weeks | Written to regional office |

### Variance Penalties
Operating without approved variance:
- Court injunction
- Civil penalties up to **$300,000**
- Notification to state/local authorities
- Immediate shutdown authority

## 5. FAA Requirements for Outdoor Shows

### Power Thresholds

| Laser Class | FAA Response |
|-------------|--------------|
| Class I, II, III (<0.5W) | Generally no objection |
| Class IV (0.5-12W) | No objection if notified in advance |
| >12W | Usually objection - extensive restrictions required |

### Notification
- Written notification 2-4 weeks in advance
- Directed to Chief of Airspace and Procedures Branch
- FAA responds within 7 days typically
- Airport proximity may trigger objections even for lower power

---

## 6. State and Local Requirements

### State Authority
- 6 states have specific laser legislation
- 25 states have authority to develop laser regulations
- All states can take action against endangering shows
- Several states have closed down non-compliant shows

### Required Information for State Notification
1. Laser safety officer name/address/phone
2. Facility manager contact information
3. Type of laser show
4. Dates and times of performance
5. Length of operation
6. Expected attendance
7. Laser class and manufacturer
8. Show layout sketches
9. FDA variance number and date (if Class IIIb/IV)

---

## 7. Individual Responsibilities

### Laser Manufacturers and Operators Must:

1. **Notify FDA** 1 month in advance (written)
2. **Contact State/Local** radiation control authorities
3. **Provide information** to facility manager
4. **Notify FAA** for outdoor shows
5. **Report accidents** to local authority AND FDA
6. **Obtain FDA variance** before using Class IIIb/IV lasers
7. **Maintain compliance** with all variance conditions

### Facility Manager Responsibilities
- Verify operator has FDA variance
- Ensure safety procedures followed
- Coordinate with authorities
- Be familiar with local safety requirements

---

## 8. Key Safety Principles

### The Fundamental Rule
> "The mere presence of a high powered laser does not necessarily pose a hazard. Scanning safeguards and other means can be taken to protect people from laser hazards."

### Three Critical Facts
1. Beam can be dangerous even when reflected
2. High-power lasers dangerous even off rough surfaces
3. Injury occurs in a fraction of a second

### Safety Hierarchy
1. **Avoidance** - Use safer alternatives if possible
2. **Engineering Controls** - Scanning, safeguards, barriers
3. **Administrative Controls** - SOPs, training, supervision
4. **PPE** - Last line of defense
