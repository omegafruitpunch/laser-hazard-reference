SKIN HAZARDS OF LASER RADIATION

While ocular injuries receive the most attention in laser safety, significant 
skin hazards exist, particularly for:
- High-power Class 4 lasers
- Ultraviolet laser systems
- Medical laser procedures
- Research and industrial laser operations

SKIN STRUCTURE RELEVANT TO LASER HAZARDS:

Epidermis (outer layer, 0.1-0.2 mm thick):
- Stratum corneum: Dead cells, provides some UV protection
- Basal layer: Contains melanocytes (melanin production)
- Melanin absorbs UV and visible light, provides protection

Dermis (1-4 mm thick):
- Contains blood vessels, nerves, hair follicles
- Collagen and elastin fibers
- Major site of thermal injury

Subcutaneous Tissue:
- Fat and connective tissue
- Deep penetration possible with certain wavelengths

Laser skin effects depend on wavelength, power density, exposure duration, 
and skin pigmentation.

MECHANISMS OF LASER SKIN INJURY

THERMAL EFFECTS:
Most common mechanism for infrared and visible lasers.
- Protein denaturation at 45-50°C
- Cell death at temperatures >60°C
- Coagulation necrosis
- Collagen shrinkage in dermis
- Vaporization at very high energies

PHOTOCHEMICAL EFFECTS:
Predominant for UV wavelengths.
- DNA absorption at 250-260 nm
- Pyrimidine dimer formation
- Direct molecular bond breaking
- Free radical generation
- Photosensitization reactions

PHOTOACOUSTIC/MECHANICAL EFFECTS:
From short-pulse lasers.
- Stress confinement (pulse shorter than thermal relaxation)
- Shock wave generation
- Tissue fragmentation
- Can cause injury beyond thermal damage zone

ELECTROMECHANICAL EFFECTS:
From very short pulses (femtoseconds).
- Plasma formation
- Dielectric breakdown
- Cavitation bubble formation

ULTRAVIOLET (UV) SKIN HAZARDS

UV-A (315-400 nm):
- Penetrates to dermis
- Contributes to photoaging
- Minimal erythema (redness)
- Photosensitization with certain medications
- Cumulative effects over time

UV-B (280-315 nm):
- Partially absorbed by epidermis
- Causes sunburn (erythema)
- Tanning response
- DNA damage
- Skin cancer risk with chronic exposure

UV-C (100-280 nm):
- Strongly absorbed by stratum corneum
- High photon energy
- Used for sterilization
- Photokeratitis risk (eyes)
- Can cause severe surface burns

ACUTE UV EXPOSURE EFFECTS:
- Erythema (redness) - delayed 2-24 hours
- Edema (swelling)
- Pain and tenderness
- Blistering (severe exposure)
- Desquamation (peeling)

CHRONIC UV EXPOSURE EFFECTS:
- Photoaging (wrinkles, solar elastosis)
- Actinic keratosis (precancerous)
- Skin cancer (basal cell, squamous cell, melanoma)
- Immunosuppression

VISIBLE AND INFRARED SKIN HAZARDS

VISIBLE LIGHT (400-700 nm):
- Primarily thermal effects
- Melanin absorption significant for darker skin
- Hemoglobin absorption bands (418, 542, 577 nm)
- Used therapeutically (PDT - Photodynamic Therapy)
- Hazard increases with power density

NEAR-INFRARED (700-1400 nm):
- Deep tissue penetration
- Water absorption increases with wavelength
- Can cause significant thermal injury
- Nd:YAG (1064 nm): Used for hair removal, skin treatment
- Risk of deep burns

MID-INFRARED (1400-3000 nm):
- Stronger water absorption
- More superficial heating
- Er:YAG (2940 nm): Strong water absorption, superficial ablation
- CO (~5000 nm): Water absorption peak

FAR-INFRARED (>3000 nm):
- CO2 laser (10,600 nm): Very strong water absorption
- Superficial effect (0.1-0.2 mm)
- Precision cutting and ablation
- Lower risk of deep thermal damage

HAZARD INCREASES WITH:
- Longer exposure duration
- Higher irradiance
- Smaller beam spot size (less heat dissipation)

SKIN MPE VALUES (Maximum Permissible Exposure)

UV RADIATION (180-400 nm):
Daily exposure limits (8-hour workday):

UV-C (180-280 nm): 3 mJ/cm²
UV-B (280-302 nm): 3 mJ/cm²  
UV-B (303 nm): 4 mJ/cm²
UV-B (304 nm): 6 mJ/cm²
UV-B (305 nm): 10 mJ/cm²
UV-B (306 nm): 16 mJ/cm²
UV-B (307 nm): 25 mJ/cm²
UV-B (308 nm): 40 mJ/cm²
UV-B (309 nm): 63 mJ/cm²
UV-B (310 nm): 100 mJ/cm²
UV-B (311 nm): 160 mJ/cm²
UV-B (312 nm): 250 mJ/cm²
UV-B (313 nm): 400 mJ/cm²
UV-B/A (314 nm): 630 mJ/cm²
UV-A (315-400 nm): 1.0 J/cm² for periods >1000 seconds

These values apply to the most sensitive (non-hyper-sensitive) skin.

VISIBLE AND IR (400-1400 nm):
- CW lasers: 0.2 W/cm² for 10 seconds to 8 hours
- Pulsed lasers: Dependent on pulse duration and repetition rate

FAR-IR (>1400 nm):
- Similar to visible range MPE values
- Less penetrating, surface effects dominate

FACTORS AFFECTING SKIN HAZARD SEVERITY

1. SKIN PIGMENTATION (FITZPATRICK SCALE):

Type I: Always burns, never tans (very fair, red hair)
- Highest laser injury risk
- Minimal melanin protection

Type II: Usually burns, tans minimally (fair)
- High laser injury risk

Type III: Sometimes burns, tans gradually (medium)
- Moderate risk

Type IV: Burns minimally, tans well (olive)
- Lower risk for visible/IR, higher for wavelengths targeting melanin

Type V: Rarely burns, tans darkly (brown)
- Can sustain higher visible/IR exposures
- Higher melanin absorption affects certain treatments

Type VI: Never burns, deeply pigmented (dark brown/black)
- Highest visible/IR tolerance
- Greater risk from melanin-specific wavelengths

2. BODY LOCATION:
- Face and neck: Thinner skin, more vulnerable
- Palms and soles: Thicker stratum corneum
- Scalp: Hair provides some protection

3. ENVIRONMENTAL FACTORS:

Ambient Temperature:
- High heat: Vasodilation increases heat dissipation
- Cold: Vasoconstriction may reduce heat dissipation

Air Movement:
- Convective cooling can reduce thermal damage

Humidity:
- Affects evaporative cooling

4. EXPOSURE PARAMETERS:

Beam Spot Size:
- Smaller spot: Less heat dissipation, higher peak temperature
- Larger spot: Better heat dissipation, lower peak temperature

Pulse Duration:
- Short pulses: Photoacoustic effects dominant
- Long pulses/continuous: Thermal effects dominant

Repetition Rate:
- High PRF: Cumulative heating
- Low PRF: Thermal relaxation between pulses

5. INDIVIDUAL FACTORS:
- Age: Skin thickness and repair capacity vary
- Medications: Photosensitizing drugs
- Previous UV damage: Compromised skin integrity
- Hydration status: Affects thermal tolerance

LASER SKIN INJURY SYMPTOMS AND CLASSIFICATION

FIRST DEGREE (Superficial):
- Erythema (redness)
- Pain
- Dry appearance
- No blistering
- Heals 3-6 days without scarring
- Similar to mild sunburn

SECOND DEGREE (Partial Thickness):
- Severe erythema
- Blister formation
- Moist, weeping surface
- Severe pain
- Heals 2-3 weeks
- May scar depending on depth

THIRD DEGREE (Full Thickness):
- White or charred appearance
- Leathery texture
- Insensate (nerve destruction)
- Destroys epidermis and dermis
- Requires medical intervention
- Scarring inevitable
- May require grafting

FOURTH DEGREE:
- Extends to subcutaneous tissue
- Muscle/bone involvement
- Black, charred appearance
- Life-threatening if extensive
- Surgical debridement required

LASER-SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS:
- High-power lasers can cause immediate third-degree burns
- UV injuries may have delayed symptoms (hours)
- Pigmentary changes (hyper or hypopigmentation) common
- Hypertrophic scarring possible

TREATMENT OF LASER SKIN INJURIES

IMMEDIATE FIRST AID:

For Thermal Burns:
1. Remove from laser source immediately
2. Cool with running water (not ice) for 10-20 minutes
3. Remove jewelry and loose clothing near affected area
4. Cover with sterile non-adherent dressing
5. Do not apply butter, oils, or creams (can trap heat)
6. Do not pop blisters
7. Seek medical attention for burns >3 inches or on face/hands

For UV Burns:
- Cool compresses
- Moisturizing lotions
- Pain relief (NSAIDs)
- Hydration
- Avoid further UV exposure

MEDICAL TREATMENT:
- Wound assessment and cleaning
- Tetanus prophylaxis if indicated
- Topical antimicrobial agents (silver sulfadiazine)
- Pain management
- Fluid resuscitation (extensive burns)
- Debridement when necessary
- Skin grafting for full-thickness burns
- Scar management (pressure therapy, silicone sheets)

LONG-TERM CARE:
- Physical therapy (for contracture prevention)
- Psychological support
- Cosmetic considerations
- Monitoring for complications

SKIN PROTECTION STRATEGIES

ENGINEERING CONTROLS:
- Enclosures and beam containment
- Interlocks on access panels
- Remote viewing via cameras
- Beam blocks and beam stops

ADMINISTRATIVE CONTROLS:
- Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)
- Controlled access to laser areas
- Training on skin hazards
- Warning signs and labels
- Buddy system for high-power operations

PERSONAL PROTECTIVE EQUIPMENT (PPE):

Gloves:
- Required for UV and high-power laser work
- Material must be appropriate for wavelength
- Leather for IR protection
- UV-resistant materials for UV lasers
- Check for degradation and damage

Lab Coats and Clothing:
- Flame-resistant materials for high-power lasers
- Long sleeves mandatory
- High collar/neck protection
- Close-fitting cuffs
- Light colors preferred (reflect visible/IR)

Face Shields:
- For protection against back-reflections
- Must be wavelength-specific
- Not a substitute for laser eyewear

Barrier Creams:
- Limited effectiveness
- May provide some UV protection
- Not a primary control method

HOUSEKEEPING:
- Eliminate reflective surfaces
- Non-reflective tools and surfaces
- Proper storage of lasers when not in use
